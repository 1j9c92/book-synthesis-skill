# Peronalized Book Synthesis Template

This document defines the content structure and voice guidelines for every book synthesis. Read this alongside `visual-principles.md`.

---

## Core Principle

**This is not a summary. It is a translation.**

A summary tells the reader what the book says. A translation tells the reader what the book means — for them, in their domain, given their current problems and goals. Every example, every analogy, every "so what" should be unrecognizable to someone reading a generic book summary. If the reader's domain isn't in every paragraph, you haven't translated.

---

## Opening: Why This Book, Why Now

Before any ideas, write a single opening section (3–5 paragraphs maximum) that answers:

1. **Why is this book relevant to this reader right now?** — Not generically relevant. Specifically relevant. Name the reader's current situation and connect it directly to what the book addresses.
2. **What is the book actually about?** — The real argument, not the marketing description.
3. **What will this synthesis give the reader that a summary wouldn't?** — Name the applications this reader should expect.

Write this section in plain, direct prose. No headers within it. Think of it as the framing that earns the reader's attention for everything that follows.

---

## Idea Structure (repeat for each of the 5–10 ideas)

Each idea section has exactly these components — in this order:

### 1. Idea Statement (1–2 sentences)
State the idea clearly. Not a quote from the book. Your synthesis of the core claim.

### 2. The Principle (Why It's True)
Explain the underlying logic — why this is true, not just what it is. This is the most important part and the most skipped. A good principle section makes the idea feel inevitable, not arbitrary.

Target: 2–4 paragraphs.

### 3. Domain Visual
Here, insert the appropriate visual element (from visual-principles.md) that illustrates the concept. Choose the visual type that matches the concept's structure:
- Sequential steps → flow diagram
- Cause-and-effect loop → circular loop diagram
- Comparison → side-by-side comparison card
- 4-part framework → 4-card grid
- Ranking/hierarchy → horizontal bar chart
- Decision logic → decision tree
- Timeline/plan → timeline grid

The visual should illustrate the principle, not decorate it. A reader should be able to understand the concept from the visual alone.

### 4. In Your Domain
Replace the book's examples with examples drawn from the reader's specific domain(s) from their profile. Be specific. Reference actual workflows, decisions, problems the reader has named.

This is the hardest section to write well. Avoid generic examples ("in any organization…"). Every sentence should be recognizable to the reader.

Target: 2–4 paragraphs.

### 5. Connected to Your Situation
Make an explicit connection between the idea and something in the reader's current goals, challenges, or growth edges. This is personal — draw from the motivation/growth and blind spots sections of the reader profile.

Write in second person ("you," "your"). Be direct. This section should feel like the author wrote it knowing the reader.

Target: 1–2 paragraphs.

### 6. So What (callout block)
A single, concrete action or reframe. Not a list. One thing. Specific enough that the reader could do it today or this week without needing more information.

Format as a So What callout block (see visual-principles.md).

---

## Closing: What to Do Next

End every synthesis with 3–5 concrete experiments — not homework, not aspirations. These are specific, achievable actions with a clear "done" state.

Format as a timeline grid (Week 1–2 / Month 1 / Month 2–3) when there are 4+ actions. For 3 actions, use numbered action cards instead.

Each experiment should:
- Have a specific done state (not "think about X" but "write down X and share it with Y")
- Connect to a specific idea from the synthesis
- Be achievable without additional information or permissions

End with the closing card (see visual-principles.md) — one sentence that captures the book's core insight in the reader's context.

---

## Voice Guidelines

Match the reader's cognitive style from their profile:

**If framework-first (a):** Lead with the model or framework, then give examples. Use diagrams early in each idea section — before the prose explanation.

**If story-first (b):** Open each idea with a specific scenario or case study from the reader's domain, then extract the principle from it.

**If checklist/playbook (c):** Use numbered steps, explicit checklists, and "if X then Y" formats throughout. The "so what" should always be a specific step with a sequence.

**If visual-first (d):** Every idea opens with its diagram before any prose. Maximize visual density — more diagrams, more callout blocks, less unbroken text.

## Length Targets

| Length | Words | Ideas | Avg per idea | Use when |
|--------|-------|-------|--------------|----------|
| Short  | ~3,000 | 5–6 | 500 words | Dense practical books, quick reads |
| Medium | ~5,000 | 7–8 | 600–700 words | Most business books |
| Long   | ~10,000 | 9–10 | 1,000+ words | Dense theoretical works, when deep application is needed |

Default to the reader's stated preference. Cut anything that doesn't land specifically for this reader — a shorter, sharper synthesis always wins.

---

## Quality Checklist (run before finalizing)

- [ ] Every example is drawn from the reader's domain(s) — no generic examples remain
- [ ] Every idea has at least one purpose-built visual element
- [ ] Every idea has a domain application section, a personal connection, and a so what
- [ ] The opening section names the reader's specific situation and connects it to the book
- [ ] The closing action plan has specific done states, not aspirations
- [ ] The visual vocabulary is consistent (same callout colors for same types throughout)
- [ ] No blank pages or large white-space voids
- [ ] Length matches reader's stated preference
- [ ] Voice matches reader's stated cognitive style
- [ ] The one-sentence closing card captures the book's core insight in the reader's context
